The project spanned four months, through which we were working on weekly assignments to progress with our problem, understand it well, and come up with a solution. Pal

Integration of Newcomers and Locals.

************************************************************************************
Winner of:
EIT Digital Summer School Business Challenge,
Business Ideation Weekend by The Shortcut,
and Digital Prototyping Weekend by The Shortcut
************************************************************************************


 Zuka was having a good life before she had to move from her city, Aleppo, on a 20-day journey to Sweden. And none of what she used to do back home is possible to practice anymore...



Above is the story of my cousin who was a living a normal life until she had to leave her country Syria due to the unpleasant situation. After arriving to Sweden, Zuka faced a lot of barriers hindering her settlement and return to the life she had. She had to wait for a year to get her residence status to start learning the language, then getting some practical work, then going back on track with a job.

With 160,000 people like Zuka arriving only to Sweden in 2015, our team felt that there is a great need to target in our Summer School in Paris, which was themed around Cyber-Physical Systems, and part of several summer schools held by the EIT Digital Master School. Our project gained attendtion by the summer school supervisors, since it tackles a huge societal problem.

Pal was our solution for refugees to integrte with locals based on sharing their stories. Locals love to help, but find trusting strangers difficult. Pal aims to build trust by showing that these new-comers are just like the locals, with talents and great potential. The differentiating factor is the societal interaction that Pal provides to locals, making them already know about the refugee before even meeting them inperson to help them.

The name comes from the fact that refugees and locals are like pals (friends), and also serves as an acronym for "Peace and love".

LettuceMine

Analytics and Visualization of people's tweets and sentiments.

The Business Development Lab, one of the most memorable courses that I took at KTH in Stockholm, was the birth of this startup idea involving big data, visualization, and social media analytics. Five months was the time given for each team to found a business from conception to having real partnerships and customers. Having worked on the business model from bottom to top, starting with the value proposition, to the customer segments and customer relations, to the partnerships and revenue stream, we had a great chance experiencing the startup world with its stressful deadlines, painful rejections, but joyful partnerships.

Our idea was to help data journalists and brand analysts to get better visualized insights from tweets, including which areas of the world are tweeting about a topic, which topic is predicted to trend, as well as the sentiments associated with the tweets. This enables both journalists to be on the frontline in writing stories and predict potential trends, and englighten brand anaysts with what people feel about their brand. Since we promise that using our service we mine insights from the pool of big data, we called it "LettuceMine". Moreover, lettuce had a special significance among our team. LettuceMine's business model was based on add-ons, where customers pay for the features they choose from the platform, as well as choose certain key-terms whose trendability (if it's a term :P) is determined by the service. The theme also expanded to maintaining customer relations by sending our subscribers, whom we called "miners", a weekly "data salad", which is a collection of trening topics and visualized insights about them.


In the end, we agreed on partnership with Gavagai, which provides the sentiment analysis from the tweets we collate. As for the customers, high interests from journalists in Sweden's Aftonbladet and The Local, and brand analysts from Vattenfall.

Future Bus Station - Juncton 2016

Targeted Advertisements Based on People's Age and Gender.

************************************************************************************
Winner of the IoT Track of Junction 2016 Hackathon
************************************************************************************


In Europe's largest hackathon, Junction 2016, held in Helsinki, our team was interested in the IoT track. We wanted to think of solving a problem in the current bus stops and find a solution for it within two days. Upon ideation, we came up with a solution that benefits both the people waiting and the companies that are adversitising.

The idea was to show advertisements based on the people's gender and age, rather than having a static set of ads for all. Through detecting facial expressions of ad viewers (sad, smiling, laughing, etc.), we can have better information on their feelings towards the ads, which is valuable for making more effective ad campaigns.

From the people's perspective, ads will be more relevant to their gender and age range, since a middle-aged man would probably care more about beer and sports-bar ads than cosmetics ads, for instance. At the same time, people waiting in the stop have an ineractive information portal that they can use to check their route in a live map, view the news or weather, or indicate that they want a certain bus to stop (instead of having to actively wait and raise their hands).

Wirelessly Steered Robotc Arm

Computer Engineering Design Project



Here was where I put my four years of knowledge into practice. In four months, with a team of two Electrical Engineering students, we ideated, researched, designed, and implemented a wirelessly steered robotic arm. Below is information from the project's abstract:

The need for the robotics to aid people in different daily tasks is increasing. Today, building a robotic arm to flex and extend its fingers, roll, and pitch similar to a human’s arm simultaneously, with the use of an augmented glove worn by a person, is possible.

This can work by the glove being fit with sensors to sense the human arm motion and action, the robotic arm with strings and actuators, and a control unit on each side to record the state of the human arm and actuate the servo motors to move accordingly.

The sensors used in the augmented glove include a flex sensors on each finger to detect the finger flexion and an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) on the wrist to detect the rolling and pitching of the arm. The actuators on the robotic arm include a low-torque servo motors attached to a string that is tied to each finger for the flexion and extension, and two high-torque servo motors to do the rolling and pitching.

The two control units that are used on each of the augmented steering glove and the robotic arm include Arduino Mega and Arduino Uno, respectively. Both microcontrollers communicate with each other wirelessly through ZigBee technology, using an XBee transceiver each to transmit the data from the steering glove microcontroller to the robotic arm microcontroller.

With the wireless communication achieved between the two microcontrollers, the robotic arm can be steered within a range of a 100 meters, and can be used for several applications. Currently useful for handling simple light objects, the system serves as a proof of concept for systems being used in larger applications like prosthetics or handling hazardous material in harsh environments.

My individual work included the initial design of the system, enhancing the finger flexion, the wireless communication using the XBee transceivers, and the 3D design and printing of the final iteration of the prototype.

Implementing Parallellized DNA Sequencing Algorithms

Field Experience in Computer Engineering

In the summer of my third year at AUD, I performed my field experience in computer engineering at the computer engineering lab of Delft University of Technology (TU Delft). Located in Delft, The Netherlands, the university is one of the leading technology and research universities in Europe.

Research on improving bioinformatics algorithms (by parallelizing them) and implementing them on different suprcomputing platforms to ensure high performance from both software and hardware. The improved algorithms by computer engineers can hopefully help the bioinformaticians to recognize flaws in genetic data. This in turn would help the molecular biologists (and hence physicians) to find better solutions to address various health issues such as cancer.

This made me excited to work on the project, since I could see the big picture of computer engineering and put it in a real-life context. More details of what I did in those three months can be found here.

Lessons learned: Working individually and learning new things, as well as learning how to learn them, was practiced extensively. Learning topics and concepts that require background from molecular biology was not trivial, especially when deciding what information is necessary. Hence, the way of learning was developed into going to the breadth of the topic first, and then to the depth of its parts as necessary, while building assumptions throughout the process. Equally important are the lessons learned while experiencing the nature of doing research, such as working to create benchmarks.

Microprocessors Project



One of the core courses of computer engineering during my bachelors was Microprocessors. Here is where I learned and practiced low-level programming. The course project involved using the instructions of an 8085 microprocessor in a code, as well as the come up with the hardware, in order to implement a real life application, which is a parking counter.
Lessons learned:
We were able to come up with both the program and the hardware of the system by using arithmetic and logical operations, delay loops, subroutines, and hardware configurations. Particularly for this project, we have seen how there is more than one way to solve a problem and build a system since we were not able to use interrupts due to limitations but have used continuous looping instead. Student Leadership

Various Student Organization Experiences


From day one at university, I was determined to get involved in the student life to build my personality, crush my comfort zone, and nurture my leadership skills. Soon I became part of the founding team of the university's Peer Health Educators, the region's first student group affiliated with UNICEF and acts as a consultant for health topics such as HIV/AIDS. Considering that spreading awareness is a value of huge importance to me, I remained a PHE for the whole four-year period at my university.

The second year I became the treasurer of AUD's IEEE student branch, organizing various seminars and fun activities for students. I also became an active orientation leader, working with other fellow students and the Student Government Association (SGA), the mother of all student organizations, to help new students feel home and tackle their issues.

The final two years I became a SGA's treasurer and then elected by the student body as its president. That was indeed the most challenging and fun position, in which our team was able to work with the university's administration to tackle problems faced by the student body, as well as organize events that bring life to the university, most notably AUD's annual International Night.

Besides leadership positions, I was an active member in several cultural clubs, representing them on the stage in various signing and dancing performances, including International Night Performances, The Voice AUD, and Syrian, Palestenian, and Lebanese Nights.

Those were the days that made me who I am today. Handling these challenging positions while studying engineering helped me to evolve as a leader, and later on as a successful young professional.
